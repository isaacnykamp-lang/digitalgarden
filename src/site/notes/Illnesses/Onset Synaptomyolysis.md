*An illness originating from extreme electrical discharges usually caused by geomagnetically enhanced storms. While lightning strikes on human bodies are more common, they are yet rare, and rarer still are incumbent diseases and neuroses that can manifest after the fact. Synaptomyolysis is more commonly called Strike Syndrome, the sufferer is frequently ailed by persistent, unpredictable atonic-clonic seizures. These fits strengthen over time, with the only outlook being terminal within the half decade or earlier.

*Symptoms will often remain dormant and go without notice until the point of terminal outlook, presenting as pins and needles, the appearance of iron deficiency, absence seizures and other dysregulations that are quickly remedied by natural process. Detecting individuals impaired by Strike Syndrome in a reversible stage is difficult to the extent of near impossibility, with miniscule epigenetic changes being the only marker.

*The symptoms themselves can be medicated until a point, but neural degeneration will activate what are essentially full body cramps as the chemical processes for regulating electric input slowly fry themselves. Sufferers will need to ensure that their fits are not so strong that they suffocate or develop tachycardia and other cardiac arrhythmias with prolonged exposure. Simple potassium based counter-measures are available for prolonging general health, but there is no cure and the dysbiotic process gradually wears down the body until a seizure guarantees fatality– be it kidney and liver damage from rhabdomyolysis of misfiring muscles, cardiac asystole, vasovagal syncope, cranial suffocation from degenerating nerves, Strike Syndrome aggravates the body towards invariable systemic failure. Accounts from sufferers liken it to being struck by lightning repeatedly with increasing strength, followed by post-ictal periods marked with extreme weakness and fatigue.

*“It fools you into thinking it’s manageable. You never notice when the seizures are bouts of deja vu or spacing out every once in a while. When the lightning strikes, you feel your hairs stand on end before it happens. Plenty of time to react. But by then? Too late. It’s going to catch you eventually.”
